## ü§ì  Background research

Before we start, what exactly is "muscle memory"? 



Muscle memory is the ability to perform movements automatically without consciously thinking about each step. According to the Cleveland Clinic, this form of memory is **not actually stored in the muscles**, but in the brain ‚Äî specifically in the **motor cortex, cerebellum, and striatum**, which handle procedural (implicit) memory.

When you repeatedly practice a movement, your muscles adapt by increasing **myonuclei** inside muscle fibers. These extra nuclei remain even after you stop training, allowing muscles to regain size and strength more quickly when you resume ‚Äî this is why retraining is much faster than starting from zero.

Muscle memory develops in **three stages**:

1. **Cognitive Phase** ‚Äî you think through each step.
2. **Associative Phase** ‚Äî performance improves with practice.
3. **Autonomous Phase** ‚Äî the movement becomes automatic.

Most importantly, the studies suggest that **previously trained muscles regain size and strength significantly faster due to retained myonuclei.**



**Citation:**
 *Cleveland Clinic. ‚ÄúMuscle Memory: What It Is & How It Works.‚Äù Last reviewed Feb. 19, 2025.*

**Critical Factors for This Outcome**

This hypothesis depends on three key factors that I must control for in the experiment:

1. **Caloric Surplus:** I cannot regain 9.2 kg of mass (from 61 kg back to 70.2 kg) without eating in a consistent caloric surplus. the strength gains will stall if my bodyweight doesn't increase, as the peak strength us tied to the peak mass.

2. **High Protein Intake:** I must consume adequate protein (e.g., 1.6-2.2 grams per kg of bodyweight) to provide the building blocks for muscle repair and growth.

3. **Consistency & Progressive Overload:** I must stick to the routine and focus on **progressive overload**‚Äîadding a little more weight or perhaps an extra rep whenever possible.
